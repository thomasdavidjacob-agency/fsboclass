MODULE 10

Handling Inspections & Appraisals

📗 Complete Course⏱ 30 min read🏠 Module 10 of 12

Inspections and appraisals are the two most common points where real estate transactions stall, fall apart, or result in significant renegotiation. As an FSBO seller, you'll navigate both without an agent buffering the process. That's not a disadvantage — it means you can respond directly, decisively, and with a full understanding of what the findings actually mean. This module gives you the frameworks to handle both with confidence.

Part 1: The Home Inspection

What Inspectors Actually Look For

A licensed home inspector is a generalist who evaluates the visible and accessible components of a residential property and identifies conditions that appear deficient, safety-related, or beyond normal wear. Inspectors typically examine:

  • Roof: Condition of shingles, flashing, gutters, and fascia. Evidence of leaks or improper installation.
  • Foundation and structure: Visible cracks, settlement, moisture intrusion, framing issues in accessible areas.
  • Electrical system: Panel condition, wiring type (aluminum wiring is flagged in older homes), GFCI outlets in required locations, grounding, safety hazards.
  • Plumbing: Water pressure, visible pipe conditions, drainage, water heater age and condition, signs of leaks.
  • HVAC: Heating and cooling system age, condition, and operation. Duct condition where accessible.
  • Insulation and ventilation: Attic insulation levels, ventilation adequacy, signs of moisture damage.
  • Windows and doors: Condition, operation, sealing, evidence of moisture between panes.
  • Exterior: Siding condition, grading (does water drain away from the foundation?), decks and porches.
  • Interior: Walls, ceilings, floors — visible evidence of water damage, staining, or structural issues.

Understanding Inspection Report Language

Inspection reports can be alarming to read if you don't understand how inspectors categorize findings. Most reports use language like:

  • "Safety concern" or "immediate action required": Genuine safety issues like exposed wiring, gas leaks, or significant structural problems. These are the items that require attention.
  • "Recommend repair/replacement": Items that are deficient and should be addressed, but are not immediate safety hazards. These are negotiating points.
  • "Monitor": Items worth watching but not immediately actionable. Often used for minor cracks, early-stage wear, or conditions that may develop into larger issues.
  • "Noted" or "For your information": Items the inspector is flagging for awareness that don't require action. Normal maintenance items often fall here.

A 60-item inspection report sounds alarming. But if 50 of those items are "monitor" and "noted" notations — caulk the window trim, clean the dryer vent, replace the furnace filter — that's a normal report on a maintained home. Focus your attention and negotiation energy on the safety concerns and significant repair recommendations, not the entire document.

💡 The Pre-Listing Inspection Advantage

If you complete your own pre-listing inspection before going on the market, you already know what's in the report — and you've either fixed the issues or priced accordingly. When the buyer's inspection comes in, it has no surprises for you. This dramatically reduces post-inspection renegotiation anxiety and positions you as a transparent, prepared seller.

Responding to an Inspection Report

After the buyer submits their inspection response (requesting repairs, credits, or price reduction), you have three options for each requested item:

Option 1: Agree and Repair

You agree to complete specific repairs before closing. Use licensed contractors, get everything in writing, and provide receipts at closing. Never perform significant repairs yourself unless you're genuinely qualified — a buyer can request proof of contractor work, and amateur repairs can create liability.

Best for: Safety items, items that would fail a lender's property condition requirements, and repairs where you can control the cost by getting your own competitive bids.

Option 2: Offer a Credit

Instead of making repairs yourself, you offer a dollar amount at closing that the buyer can use for repairs after they take ownership. Credits are often preferable to repairs: you don't have to manage contractors on a tight timeline, the buyer gets to choose their own contractor, and the transaction moves forward without repair delays.

When offering a credit: use actual contractor quotes where possible — don't just accept the buyer's numbers. Get 1–2 bids for significant items and offer the lower estimate. "I've received quotes of $3,200–$3,800 for the roof repair. I'm offering a $3,200 closing credit for this item."

Option 3: Decline

You can decline to repair or credit any inspection item. This terminates the negotiation on that point and the buyer must decide whether to proceed as-is, exit the contract (if within their inspection contingency window), or come back with a revised request. Declining is not the end of the deal — it's a negotiating position.

Best for: Items you believe are normal wear, items already factored into your price, items where the buyer's request is disproportionate to the actual repair cost, and situations where you've already disclosed the item.

What NOT to Concede On

Buyers (and their agents) sometimes use inspection reports to renegotiate price on items that have nothing to do with genuine defects. Watch for:

  • Requests based on items you already disclosed — they knew about it when they made the offer
  • Cosmetic issues (paint, carpet, caulk lines) bundled into a repair request
  • Items that appear on inspection reports of virtually every home of your age (older panel brands, older-style windows, etc.)
  • Requests where the amount is significantly disproportionate to actual repair cost — always get your own quotes

Special Inspections

Beyond the general home inspection, buyers may request or lenders may require specialized inspections. Know what each involves:

  • Termite/WDO inspection: Inspects for wood-destroying organisms. Many lenders require this. If evidence of active infestation is found, treatment is typically required before closing.
  • Radon test: Measures radon gas levels. EPA action level is 4 pCi/L. Mitigation systems run $800–$2,500. Common requirement in high-radon areas.
  • Sewer scope: Camera inspection of the main sewer line. Increasingly requested in older homes. Issues found can be significant — lateral line replacement runs $3,000–$10,000.
  • Mold test: Air quality testing for mold spores. Required when visible mold is noted in the general inspection or disclosed by the seller.
  • Septic inspection: Required for homes on septic systems. Includes pump-out and system evaluation.

Part 2: The Appraisal

For financed purchases, the buyer's lender orders an independent appraisal after the inspection period. The appraiser's job is to provide the lender with a professional opinion of the property's market value — because the lender is lending against the property's value, not the agreed purchase price.

How the Appraiser Works

The appraiser will schedule an appointment (typically 30–60 minutes at the property) to measure the home, note its condition, document features, and take photos. They'll then complete a desk review comparing your home to 3–6 comparable sales and arrive at an opinion of value. This process takes 5–14 days from the appointment to the delivery of the report to the lender.

If the Appraisal Comes In At Value

Great — the lender will fund the loan at the agreed price and you proceed to closing. This is the most common outcome in a well-priced transaction.

If the Appraisal Comes In Short

An appraisal that values the home below the purchase price (called a "low appraisal" or "appraisal gap") creates a problem: the lender will only lend up to the appraised value. For example, if you're under contract at $420,000 but the home appraises at $400,000, the lender will only fund based on $400,000. The $20,000 gap must be resolved. Your options:

  • Reduce the purchase price to the appraised value — the most common resolution. You take $20,000 less than agreed.
  • Ask the buyer to pay the gap in cash — the buyer brings an additional $20,000 to closing above what the lender funds. Only works if the buyer has the cash and motivation.
  • Split the gap — negotiate a price somewhere between the appraised value and the original purchase price. Both parties absorb part of the shortfall.
  • Challenge the appraisal — submit a formal reconsideration of value (ROV) with comparable sales data that supports your price. This is worth attempting if you have solid comps the appraiser missed or used incorrectly.
  • Walk away — if no resolution is possible and the buyer had an appraisal contingency, they can exit the contract and recover their earnest money.

How to Avoid a Low Appraisal

  • Price your home accurately using solid comps — overpriced homes appraise short
  • Prepare a comp package for the appraiser: a one-page summary of 3–4 recent comparable sales that support your price, with addresses and sale prices. Appraisers are required to consider this information.
  • Be present during the appraisal to answer questions and highlight features the appraiser might not immediately notice (recent mechanical updates, permits pulled, custom features)
  • Ensure the home is clean and well-presented — condition affects the appraiser's qualitative assessment
📋 Challenging an Appraisal

If you believe the appraisal contains errors — wrong square footage, a comparable sale that isn't actually comparable, an important recent sale that was excluded — you can request a Reconsideration of Value through the buyer's lender. You cannot contact the appraiser directly (this is prohibited). Provide documentation and comparable sales data to the buyer, who submits it to the lender for the ROV.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • A 60-item inspection report is normal — focus only on safety concerns and significant repair recommendations, not the full list
  • For each inspection request, you have three options: repair, credit, or decline — credits are often preferable to repairs for timeline and control
  • Always get your own contractor quotes before offering credits — never simply accept the buyer's cost estimates
  • A low appraisal creates an appraisal gap with four resolution options: price reduction, buyer pays gap, split the gap, or challenge the appraisal
  • Prepare a comp package for the appraiser — a one-page summary of supporting comps you provide helps ensure they have the full picture
  • A pre-listing inspection eliminates inspection surprises and positions you as a transparent, prepared seller

✅ Action Steps

  1. Consider scheduling a pre-listing inspection now — the $300–$500 cost routinely pays for itself in reduced post-contract negotiation.
  2. Get contractor quotes for any known issues in advance — having quotes ready makes inspection response decisions fast and informed.
  3. Prepare your comp packet for the appraiser: 3–4 recent sold comps that best support your list price, with addresses, sale prices, and square footage.
  4. Know your walk-away number on inspection requests — decide in advance how much total concession (repairs + credits) you're willing to make before you'd rather walk.
  5. Read Module 7 (Negotiation Tactics) alongside this module — inspection negotiation is an extension of offer negotiation and the same principles apply.

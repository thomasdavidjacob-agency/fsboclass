Legal Disclosures by State

📗 Complete Course⏱ 30 min read🏠 Module 9 of 12

Seller disclosure is one of the most legally consequential parts of selling a home — and one of the most misunderstood. Failing to disclose a known material defect can expose you to lawsuits years after closing. But disclosure doesn't mean you have to confess every scratch and squeaky floorboard. It means providing the information buyers need to make an informed decision about a property whose condition only you — as the current owner — truly know. This module explains exactly what you're required to disclose, what you're not, how AS-IS sales work, and where to find your state's specific forms.

What Is Seller Disclosure?

Seller disclosure is the legal obligation to inform buyers of material facts about a property that could affect its value or a buyer's decision to purchase. A "material fact" is anything a reasonable buyer would consider important — not necessarily every minor imperfection, but certainly anything that would meaningfully affect the property's value, safety, or the buyer's enjoyment of it.

Disclosure requirements vary significantly by state. Some states (California, for example) have comprehensive 11-page disclosure forms covering dozens of specific items. Others have minimal formal requirements and rely more on the general common-law principle that sellers must disclose known material defects. Understanding what your state requires is essential — and non-compliance isn't excused by ignorance.

The Universal Federal Disclosure: Lead Paint

Regardless of your state's specific requirements, one disclosure is federally mandated for virtually all residential sales: the EPA Lead-Based Paint Disclosure.

If your home was built before 1978, federal law (the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act) requires you to:

  • Provide buyers with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family from Lead in Your Home"
  • Disclose any known lead-based paint hazards in the home
  • Provide any available records or reports related to lead paint (prior inspections, remediation records)
  • Give buyers a 10-day window to conduct a lead paint inspection or risk assessment before the contract becomes binding (buyers can waive this right in writing)

This disclosure applies to all sellers — FSBO and agent-represented alike — and failure to comply can result in fines up to $10,000 per violation plus potential civil liability. The disclosure form is available for free from the EPA website.

⚠️ Lead Paint Disclosure Is Non-Negotiable

This is federal law with real enforcement teeth. If your home was built before 1978, complete the Lead-Based Paint Disclosure form before accepting any offer. It takes five minutes and protects you from significant legal exposure.

What You Must Disclose: The General Standard

Across virtually all states, the core disclosure obligation is the same even where specific forms differ: you must disclose any known material defect that would not be readily discoverable by a buyer through reasonable inspection.

Examples of Items That Must Be Disclosed

  • Known roof leaks or prior water intrusion, even if repaired
  • Foundation problems, settling, or structural issues
  • History of flooding, drainage problems, or basement water intrusion
  • Mold — current presence or known prior remediation
  • Pest infestations (termites, rodents) — current or prior
  • Known HVAC, electrical, or plumbing deficiencies
  • Unpermitted additions or improvements
  • Deaths that occurred in the home (requirements vary by state — some require disclosure, many do not)
  • Known environmental hazards: underground storage tanks, proximity to contaminated sites
  • Neighborhood nuisances that affect the property: noise, odors, zoning changes in progress
  • Homeowner Association disputes or pending special assessments
  • Boundary disputes or easements that affect use of the property

The "You Know It" Standard

A critical nuance: disclosure applies to what you know, not what a perfect investigation would uncover. You're not required to hire specialists to discover issues you have no reason to suspect. But if you know your basement floods every spring, you cannot simply not mention it because you fixed the immediate damage. The history of the defect — even if repaired — is generally material information.

What You Are NOT Required to Disclose

Many sellers over-disclose out of anxiety, which can create problems rather than prevent them. Here's what the law generally does not require you to disclose:

  • Conditions you genuinely didn't know about — you can only disclose what you know
  • Minor cosmetic defects that are visible and obvious
  • In most states: whether the home was the site of a crime, suicide, or natural death (check your specific state — some require disclosure of murders)
  • Whether the home is considered "haunted" or has a psychological stigma (most states explicitly exempt this)
  • Information about neighbors (fair housing rules limit what sellers can discuss about neighborhood demographics)
  • Defects you had professionally repaired with documented evidence — though the history of the defect itself may still need disclosure

State-by-State Disclosure Overview

While state-specific forms change and you should always consult your state's current requirements, here is a general framework by region:

High-Disclosure States (Comprehensive Forms Required)

California: The California Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) is one of the most comprehensive in the country — sellers must complete a detailed multi-page form covering every known physical condition of the property. California also requires a Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD) report, which must be purchased from a third-party provider.

Texas: The Texas Seller's Disclosure Notice covers dozens of specific items. Texas is a "let the buyer beware" state historically, but the disclosure form is comprehensive for what it does require.

New York: The NY Property Condition Disclosure Statement is required — though sellers may opt to provide a $500 credit to the buyer in lieu of completing it (most buyers and their attorneys insist on the form anyway).

Florida: Florida requires disclosure of all known material defects and has specific requirements around sinkholes, Chinese drywall, and flood zone status.

Moderate-Disclosure States

Oregon: Oregon's Seller Property Disclosure Statement covers property condition, environmental issues, title issues, and neighborhood conditions. FSBO sellers must complete it before accepting an offer.

Washington: The Washington Seller Disclosure Statement is required for most residential sales and covers physical condition, title, neighborhood, and environmental issues.

Colorado: Colorado uses the Seller's Property Disclosure form, which is extensive but largely based on actual knowledge rather than requiring investigation.

Limited-Disclosure States

Alabama, Arkansas, North Dakota, Wyoming: These states have minimal formal disclosure requirements and operate more under common-law principles. "Caveat emptor" (buyer beware) traditions are stronger here, but the duty to disclose known material defects still exists as a matter of law.

AS-IS Sales: What They Mean and What They Don't

An AS-IS sale does not eliminate your disclosure obligations. This is one of the most persistent misconceptions in real estate. Here's what AS-IS actually means:

What AS-IS Means

An AS-IS sale signals that you are not willing to make repairs or provide credits in response to inspection findings. You're selling the home in its current physical condition and are not agreeing upfront to fix anything. This is a negotiating position, not an exemption from disclosure law.

What AS-IS Does NOT Mean

AS-IS does not mean you can conceal known defects. You must still disclose everything you know — you're just not agreeing to address what's found. Buyers can still conduct inspections and exit the contract based on inspection findings (unless they've waived the inspection contingency). They're just put on notice that you won't negotiate repairs.

A seller who lists AS-IS and fails to disclose a known material defect is not protected by the AS-IS label. Courts have consistently held that AS-IS clauses do not excuse fraudulent concealment or intentional non-disclosure.

📋 When AS-IS Makes Sense

AS-IS sales are common in estate sales, foreclosure-adjacent situations, or when a seller knows there are significant issues and has priced accordingly. If you're selling AS-IS at a below-market price that reflects the home's condition, it's a legitimate strategy — but you still must disclose what you know.

How to Protect Yourself Legally

1. Be Thorough and Honest

When in doubt, disclose. The cost of over-disclosing (a buyer negotiates a small credit) is far lower than the cost of non-disclosure litigation. Real estate lawsuits based on failed disclosure can drag on for years and cost tens of thousands in legal fees — far more than any repair or credit would have cost.

2. Document Everything

Keep records of all repairs, inspections, and maintenance work you've had done on the home. These records support your disclosure statements and demonstrate good faith. Receipts from contractors, inspection reports, warranty documents — keep them all and provide copies to the buyer at closing.

3. Get Your Disclosure Form in Order Early

Complete your seller disclosure form before you list — not after you're under contract. Review it carefully with fresh eyes, walk through the home with the form in hand, and answer each question truthfully based on your actual knowledge. Have a family member or trusted friend review it for completeness.

4. Consider a Pre-Listing Inspection

Hiring your own inspector before you list ($300–$500) gives you a complete picture of your home's condition and eliminates surprises during the buyer's inspection. Anything the pre-listing inspection finds either gets fixed proactively or gets disclosed. This approach reduces negotiation friction after you're under contract — one of the most common deal-killers in FSBO transactions.

Where to Find Your State's Disclosure Forms

Your state's residential seller disclosure form is typically available from one of these sources:

  • Your state's real estate regulatory commission website (search "[state] real estate commission disclosure form")
  • Your state's Association of Realtors website (forms are often publicly available)
  • Your title company or closing attorney — they'll have the current standard form
  • Your flat-fee MLS service — many include disclosure forms as part of their package

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Disclosure applies to what you know — you're not required to investigate, but you cannot conceal what you're aware of
  • The federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure is required for all homes built before 1978 — no exceptions
  • AS-IS sales do not exempt you from disclosure requirements — they only signal you won't make repairs after inspection
  • Disclosure requirements vary significantly by state — California, Texas, Florida, and New York have the most comprehensive forms
  • When in doubt, disclose — the cost of a repair credit is always lower than the cost of post-closing litigation
  • A pre-listing inspection eliminates surprises and reduces post-contract negotiation friction — worth the $300–$500 investment

✅ Action Steps

  1. Download your state's current seller disclosure form and read it in full — note every question you're unsure how to answer.
  2. If your home was built before 1978, download and complete the EPA Lead-Based Paint Disclosure form from epa.gov before accepting any offer.
  3. Walk through your home with the disclosure form in hand and answer each question based on your actual knowledge — don't guess, don't minimize.
  4. Gather records of all repairs, inspections, and maintenance work done during your ownership — compile them in a folder to provide to the buyer at closing.
  5. Consider scheduling a pre-listing inspection with a licensed home inspector — the $300–$500 cost nearly always pays for itself by reducing post-contract negotiation.

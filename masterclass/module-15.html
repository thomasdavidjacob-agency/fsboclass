Working With Buyer's Agents

🏆 Masterclass⏱ 30 min read🏠 Module 15 of 16

More than 85% of buyers are represented by a buyer's agent. This means that as an FSBO seller, the majority of your showings, negotiations, and transactions will involve a licensed real estate agent on the other side of the table. Understanding how buyer's agents think, how they're compensated, what they want from you, and how to manage the relationship strategically is not optional for Masterclass sellers — it's essential. This module gives you a complete guide to navigating buyer's agent relationships to close more deals at better terms.

Understanding the Buyer's Agent's Incentive Structure

To work effectively with buyer's agents, you need to understand what motivates them — and compensation is the primary driver. Buyer's agents earn a commission only when a transaction closes. They're not paid for showings, for writing offers that don't get accepted, or for time spent with buyers who ultimately don't purchase. This creates a specific set of priorities that Masterclass sellers can use to their advantage:

What Buyer's Agents Want

  • A transaction that closes: Every buyer's agent's primary goal is to get their client into a home and close the deal. They want FSBO sellers who are organized, responsive, and capable of executing the paperwork side of the transaction smoothly.
  • Clear compensation terms upfront: After the 2024 NAR settlement, buyer's agents must now have written buyer representation agreements in place before showing homes. They want to know, before they bring a client to your door, whether you're willing to cover their commission — or whether their buyer will need to pay it out of pocket.
  • A seller who won't derail the deal: Agents prefer working with sellers who understand the process, don't create unnecessary drama over minor inspection items, and respond to communications promptly. FSBO sellers with a professional approach are valued; FSBO sellers who are difficult, unresponsive, or unreasonable are avoided.
  • Showings that are easy to schedule: If your showing process is complicated, requires 48-hour notice, or involves a difficult showing experience, agents will show your home less frequently than a comparable listing with easy access.

The 2024 NAR Settlement: What Changed for FSBO Sellers

The National Association of Realtors settlement that took effect in August 2024 changed the rules around buyer's agent compensation in significant ways that directly affect FSBO sellers. You need to understand these changes clearly.

What Changed

Prior to the settlement, it was standard practice for sellers to offer a buyer's agent commission in the MLS listing — typically 2–3% of the purchase price — which the buyer's agent would receive at closing. This was essentially mandated by MLS rules in most markets. The settlement eliminated this requirement: sellers are no longer obligated to offer any buyer's agent commission in the MLS.

Additionally, buyer's agents are now required to have signed buyer representation agreements (BRAs) with their clients before showing homes. These agreements specify how the agent will be compensated, by how much, and by whom.

What Didn't Change

You can still choose to offer buyer's agent compensation — and in many markets, it remains strategically beneficial to do so. The change is that it's now truly optional and negotiable, not an MLS requirement. Sellers can offer 0%, 1%, 2%, 2.5%, or any amount they choose. They can also negotiate buyer's agent compensation as part of the offer process.

Your Strategic Options

  • Offer 2–2.5% upfront in the MLS: Maximizes buyer's agent motivation to show your home. In a competitive market where you're offering an auction-style pricing strategy, this ensures agents actively bring clients. You're saving the listing commission (2.5–3%) and paying the buyer's side — net savings of 0–1%.
  • Offer 1–1.5% in the MLS: A compromise that shows willingness to cooperate while reducing your payout. Some agents will bring buyers at this rate; others won't. Know your market.
  • Offer nothing in the MLS, negotiate per offer: This maximizes your flexibility — you negotiate buyer's agent compensation as part of each offer's terms. Some buyers will negotiate their agent's compensation into their offer price. Others will ask you to concede it as a seller concession at closing.
⚠️ The Hidden Risk of Offering Nothing

Offering no buyer's agent compensation doesn't mean buyer's agents won't show your home — but it does mean some will not. Agents who have buyer representation agreements specifying their fee may steer clients away from your listing if they can't confirm you'll cover it. In a buyer's market where you need every showing you can get, this self-imposed obstacle can cost you significantly more than the commission you're avoiding.

First Contact: Setting the Right Tone

When a buyer's agent calls to schedule a showing, the interaction sets the tone for the entire relationship. Here's how to handle first contact professionally:

What to Say When Agents Call

Answer calls promptly and professionally. Don't answer with a casual "hello" — you're a businessperson managing a significant transaction. Your script:

"Thank you for calling about [address]. I'm happy to schedule a showing. What dates and times work best for your clients? I can typically accommodate showings [your available hours] with [your notice requirement] notice."

If the agent asks about commission: "I'm offering [X]% buyer's agent compensation. Is that something that works for your clients' situation?" Have your answer ready before the call comes.

What NOT to Say

  • Don't negotiate price on the phone before a showing
  • Don't over-explain why you're selling FSBO — agents will probe this; stay brief and professional
  • Don't share your timeline pressure, motivation, or minimum price with agents — this is negotiating information
  • Don't express anxiety or uncertainty about the process — project competence

During Showings With Buyer's Agents

When a buyer's agent brings clients to your home, your role is very different than during an unrepresented buyer showing. The agent is managing the buyer relationship — let them do it.

Best Practice: Leave the Home

Ideally, leave the property during agent-accompanied showings. Buyers cannot speak candidly with their agent when the seller is present, which inhibits the genuine evaluation that leads to offers. Buyers who cannot honestly discuss the home's pros and cons with their agent often pass — not because they don't like the home, but because they couldn't have the private conversation that would have moved them toward an offer.

If you must remain: stay in one area of the home (the backyard or garage are ideal) and do not follow the group room to room. Give them space.

Agents Who Bring Competing Buyers

Sometimes one agent may want to bring multiple buyer clients separately to your home. This is legitimate and common for popular listings. Schedule them as separate showings. Don't share information about other interested parties with any agent — "We have a lot of interest" is fine; "We have three other offers" before you actually have them is not.

Receiving Offers Through Buyer's Agents

Offers submitted through buyer's agents are typically prepared on standard state contract forms and submitted via DocuSign or similar electronic signature platforms. When you receive an offer:

  1. Acknowledge receipt promptly: "I've received the offer and will review it. I'll respond by [time] today/tomorrow."
  2. Review the full offer — not just the price. Terms, contingencies, earnest money amount, closing date, and any addenda all affect the offer's actual value and risk profile.
  3. If you have questions about the offer's terms, you can ask the buyer's agent for clarification — they are not your agent, but they can explain what their client is requesting.
  4. Respond with a counteroffer, acceptance, or rejection within the timeframe stated in the offer (typically 24–48 hours from submission).

Managing Agent Pressure Tactics

Some buyer's agents will use pressure tactics to move you toward their client's preferred terms. Understanding common tactics lets you respond calmly and strategically:

"This Is the Best Offer You'll Get"

This is nearly always an opinion, not a fact. The agent's job is to get the best outcome for their buyer, not to help you evaluate your market. Respond: "I appreciate you sharing that. I'm going to take some time to consider the offer and I'll be in touch." Never make a decision in the moment of an agent's verbal pressure.

"My Buyer Needs to Know by [Unreasonable Deadline]"

Buyers can always extend their offer deadline if they're genuinely interested. An agent pushing an artificially short deadline is attempting to prevent you from receiving competing offers. Respond: "I understand they'd like a quick response. I'll need [your reasonable time] to review this properly, which I'll have done by [time]."

"The Market Has Shifted, You Need to Be More Realistic"

This may or may not be true. If you've been on the market for 90 days with no offers, the agent may be right. If you listed 10 days ago and this is your first offer, treat the claim skeptically. Let data — comparable sales, active listing competition, showing frequency — guide your price decisions, not an agent's advocacy for their buyer.

"FSBO Transactions Are Complicated — You Should Work With an Agent"

This is a pitch, not advice. FSBO transactions are handled by title companies and closing attorneys who manage the administrative complexity. Your job is to negotiate terms and sign documents — tasks that are entirely within a prepared seller's capability.

Using a Real Estate Attorney

While you don't need a listing agent to handle the paperwork, having a real estate attorney review your contract before signing is a Masterclass-level best practice. A real estate attorney charge $300–$600 to review a purchase agreement, flag unfavorable terms, and advise you on contingency language. In some states (New York, Illinois, Georgia, South Carolina, and others), attorney involvement at closing is required by law. Even in states where it isn't required, the cost is trivial relative to the transaction size.

💡 Build a Transaction Team

Before you list, identify the professionals you'll use: a real estate attorney for contract review, a title company for closing, and a home inspector if you're doing a pre-listing inspection. Having your team in place before you receive an offer means you can move quickly when you accept one — and buyer's agents appreciate sellers who can close on schedule.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • 85%+ of buyers are represented by agents — mastering buyer's agent relationships is not optional for a successful FSBO sale
  • The 2024 NAR settlement made buyer's agent compensation fully negotiable — decide your policy before you list and communicate it consistently
  • Leave the property during agent-accompanied showings — buyers need private space with their agent to reach the genuine emotional conclusions that produce offers
  • Never share negotiating-sensitive information with buyer's agents: your timeline, motivation, minimum price, or urgency
  • Common pressure tactics (artificial deadlines, "best offer you'll get") are advocacy for the buyer — respond with calm, evidence-based reasoning, not emotional reaction
  • Engage a real estate attorney to review your purchase agreement before signing — $300–$600 for contract review is the best insurance you can buy

✅ Action Steps

  1. Decide your buyer's agent compensation policy before you list — determine the amount you'll offer in the MLS and how you'll handle agents who negotiate this as part of an offer.
  2. Identify and contact a real estate attorney in your area who handles residential transactions — get their review fee, turnaround time, and confirm they're available when you go under contract.
  3. Set up a clear showing process: your availability hours, your notice requirement (recommend 1 hour minimum, 24 hours preferred), and your contact method (call/text). Put this in your MLS showing instructions.
  4. Prepare your "first contact" script for when agents call — practice it so it sounds natural. Know your commission answer before any agent asks.
  5. Find your state's standard residential purchase agreement (available from your state's Association of Realtors or your title company) and read it fully before you list — you'll be signing one at some point and you should understand every section.

Tax Implications of Your FSBO Sale

🏆 Masterclass⏱ 38 min read🏠 Module 16 of 16

Selling your home has tax consequences that can mean the difference between keeping tens of thousands of dollars and writing a large check to the IRS. The tax rules governing home sales are specific, favorable in most cases, but filled with traps for the uninformed. This final Masterclass module covers capital gains exclusions, cost basis calculations, how the FSBO structure affects your tax position, 1031 exchanges, and the records you need to keep. This is education — not legal or tax advice. Consult a CPA or tax attorney for guidance specific to your situation.

The Section 121 Capital Gains Exclusion

The most important tax rule for home sellers is Internal Revenue Code Section 121. This provision allows you to exclude a significant portion of your capital gain from taxable income when you sell your primary residence — potentially reducing your tax bill to zero on a substantial profit.

The Exclusion Amounts

  • Single filers: Up to $250,000 of capital gain excluded from taxable income
  • Married filing jointly: Up to $500,000 of capital gain excluded from taxable income

The Ownership and Use Tests

To qualify for the Section 121 exclusion, you must pass two tests:

  • Ownership test: You owned the home for at least 2 of the 5 years preceding the sale
  • Use test: You used the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the 5 years preceding the sale

The 2 years do not need to be continuous or the same 2 years. If you owned the home for 5 years but rented it out for 2 years in the middle, you may still qualify if you lived in it as your primary residence for at least 2 of the 5 preceding years. The rules allow for a lot of flexibility — consult a CPA if your situation is complex.

How Often Can You Use It?

You can use the Section 121 exclusion once every 2 years. This means serial home sellers who upgrade frequently can potentially exclude significant gains repeatedly over a lifetime of homeownership — one of the most generous wealth-building provisions in the tax code.

Calculating Your Capital Gain

Your capital gain is not simply "sale price minus purchase price." It's calculated on your adjusted basis, which accounts for improvements, depreciation, and selling costs. Understanding your true gain (or lack thereof) is essential before closing.

Step 1: Determine Your Original Basis

Your original basis is typically your purchase price plus all closing costs you paid when you bought the home. This includes:

  • Purchase price
  • Title insurance you paid at purchase
  • Recording fees
  • Transfer taxes paid at purchase
  • Legal fees related to the purchase
  • Points paid on your mortgage that weren't deducted in the year paid

Your purchase price alone is not your basis. Every dollar of closing cost you paid at purchase increases your basis and reduces your eventual gain.

Step 2: Add Capital Improvements

Capital improvements made during your ownership increase your basis. A capital improvement is a permanent addition or upgrade that adds value to the home, extends its useful life, or adapts it to new uses. Common examples:

  • New roof, HVAC system, or water heater
  • Kitchen or bathroom remodel
  • Room addition or finished basement
  • New windows, doors, or siding
  • Landscaping improvements (permanent hardscape, irrigation systems)
  • Deck, patio, or pool construction
  • Electrical panel upgrade or major plumbing replacement

Routine maintenance and repairs do NOT increase your basis: painting, replacing carpet, fixing a leaky faucet, cleaning services. The test is "improvement" vs. "maintenance."

Step 3: Subtract Depreciation Taken (If Applicable)

If you ever rented out your home or claimed a home office deduction for the portion of your home used exclusively for business, you may have taken depreciation deductions on that portion. Depreciation taken reduces your basis by the same amount — meaning more of your gain is taxable. This is called "depreciation recapture" and is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%, even if your other long-term capital gains rates are lower.

Most primary-residence-only homeowners have not taken depreciation and can skip this step. If you ever rented the home or claimed a home office, consult a CPA — this calculation can be complex.

Step 4: Calculate Your Adjusted Basis

Adjusted Basis = Original Basis + Capital Improvements - Depreciation Taken

Step 5: Determine Your Net Sale Proceeds

Your taxable gain is calculated from your net sale proceeds, not the gross sale price. You can deduct from the sale price:

  • Real estate commissions paid (this is where FSBO saves on taxes too — lower selling costs = lower gain)
  • Closing costs paid by the seller at closing
  • Transfer taxes paid at closing
  • Title insurance paid at closing
  • Legal fees paid at closing
  • Cost of repairs required by the buyer's inspection (if paid by the seller)
  • Home staging costs
  • Advertising and marketing expenses (including your flat-fee MLS service, photography, signage)

Your FSBO marketing costs and flat-fee MLS fees are deductible selling expenses — they reduce your net sale proceeds and therefore reduce your taxable gain.

Step 6: Calculate Your Gain

Gain = Net Sale Proceeds - Adjusted Basis

If this number is below $250,000 (single) or $500,000 (married filing jointly), and you meet the ownership and use tests, your entire gain is excluded from taxable income under Section 121. You owe zero capital gains tax.

When You Don't Qualify for the Full Exclusion

If your gain exceeds the exclusion limit, or if you don't fully meet the ownership/use tests, you'll owe capital gains tax on some portion of the gain. The rate depends on how long you owned the home:

Long-Term Capital Gains (Owned 1+ Year)

Gain on property held more than 1 year qualifies for preferential long-term capital gains rates:

  • 0% for taxpayers in the 10% and 12% ordinary income brackets
  • 15% for most middle-income taxpayers
  • 20% for high-income taxpayers (taxable income above approximately $553,000 for married filers in 2024)

Additionally, the Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) of 3.8% applies to gain above the Section 121 exclusion for taxpayers with modified AGI above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly). This means the effective maximum federal rate on excess home sale gain is 23.8% for high earners.

Short-Term Capital Gains (Owned Less Than 1 Year)

Gain on property held one year or less is taxed as ordinary income — at your marginal tax rate, which can be as high as 37%. Avoid selling property you've owned for less than a year whenever possible from a tax perspective.

The 1031 Exchange: Deferring Gain on Investment Property

If you're selling a rental or investment property (not your primary residence), the Section 121 exclusion does not apply. However, you may qualify for a 1031 exchange — also called a "like-kind exchange" — which allows you to defer capital gains tax by rolling your proceeds into a replacement investment property.

How a 1031 Exchange Works

  1. You sell your investment property and the proceeds go directly to a qualified intermediary (QI) — a third party who holds the funds. You cannot touch the money.
  2. Within 45 days of closing, you must identify up to 3 potential replacement properties in writing.
  3. Within 180 days of closing, you must close on the replacement property using the exchange proceeds.
  4. The replacement property must be of equal or greater value than the relinquished property.
  5. The gain is deferred — not eliminated. When you eventually sell the replacement property without a 1031 exchange, the accumulated deferred gain becomes taxable.

1031 exchanges can be complex and have strict timeline requirements that cannot be extended (except in FEMA-declared disasters). Always work with a qualified intermediary and a CPA experienced in real estate exchanges. Starting the exchange process after you've already received sale proceeds (without a QI in place) disqualifies you.

State Income Taxes on Home Sale Gain

Federal tax is only part of the picture. Most states with an income tax also tax capital gains on home sales — and state exclusions, if they exist, are not always identical to the federal Section 121 rules. State tax rates on capital gains vary significantly:

  • No state income tax: Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, Alaska, South Dakota (no state capital gains on your sale)
  • High capital gains tax states: California (up to 13.3%), New York (up to 10.9%), New Jersey (up to 10.75%), Oregon (up to 9.9%)
  • Moderate states: Most remaining states tax capital gains at ordinary income rates, typically 3–6%

If you live in a high-tax state like California or New York and your gain significantly exceeds the federal exclusion, state tax can be a substantial additional liability. Factor your state's rate into your net proceeds projection.

Records You Need to Keep

The IRS requires you to maintain records to support your cost basis calculation. There is no statute of limitations on real estate records — keep them for as long as you own the property plus 3 years after the year of sale (in case of audit). Records to maintain:

  • Original purchase closing disclosure (HUD-1 or Closing Disclosure)
  • Receipts for all capital improvements — contractor invoices, building permits, material receipts
  • Any prior year tax returns claiming depreciation on the property
  • Records of any casualty losses or insurance reimbursements that affected your basis
  • Your sale closing disclosure (you'll receive this at closing)
  • All FSBO selling expenses: photography receipts, flat-fee MLS invoices, signage receipts, legal fees
⚠️ The Cost Basis Record-Keeping Gap

Many long-term homeowners cannot locate their original purchase documents from 10, 15, or 20 years ago. If you've lost your original closing documents, contact the title company that handled your purchase — they retain records for many years. Your county recorder's office has the recorded deed with the purchase price. A tax professional can help reconstruct your basis from available records.

Reporting the Sale on Your Tax Return

Even if your entire gain is excluded under Section 121, you may still need to report the sale. The IRS rules:

  • If your gain is fully excluded and you did NOT receive a Form 1099-S from the title company, you generally don't need to report the sale at all
  • If you received a Form 1099-S (which title companies often issue), you must report the sale on Schedule D even if your gain is fully excluded — you show the sale and the exclusion, resulting in zero taxable gain
  • If any portion of your gain is taxable, you report it on Schedule D and Form 8949

Your title company will issue a Form 1099-S to you and to the IRS reporting the gross proceeds of the sale. This generates an automatic IRS record — do not neglect to report the sale if you receive this form.

💡 Work With a CPA This Year

The year you sell your home is the year to use a CPA for your tax return even if you normally file yourself. A CPA experienced in real estate transactions will maximize your basis, ensure all selling expenses are captured, confirm you qualify for the Section 121 exclusion, and handle the reporting correctly. The cost ($400–$800 for tax preparation) is a fraction of the potential tax savings from optimizing your return.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Section 121 allows you to exclude up to $250,000 ($500,000 married) of capital gain from taxable income — you must have owned and lived in the home as a primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years
  • Your taxable gain is net sale proceeds minus adjusted basis — not simply sale price minus purchase price; capital improvements and selling costs reduce your gain
  • FSBO selling costs (photography, flat-fee MLS, signage, legal fees) are deductible selling expenses that reduce your taxable gain
  • Long-term capital gains (held 1+ year) are taxed at 0–20% federally; short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%
  • State income taxes on home sale gain vary dramatically — high-tax states like California (13.3%) and New York (10.9%) can add significantly to your total tax burden
  • Keep all improvement receipts and original purchase documents for the life of your ownership — basis records have no statute of limitations until the year of sale plus 3 years

✅ Action Steps

  1. Locate your original purchase closing disclosure and all capital improvement receipts — compile them into a single folder now so your basis calculation is ready at tax time.
  2. Calculate your estimated gain using the 6-step framework in this module: original basis + improvements - depreciation = adjusted basis; net sale proceeds - adjusted basis = estimated gain.
  3. Confirm whether you meet the Section 121 ownership and use tests — if your situation is complex (rental period, partial use, recent purchase), consult a CPA before closing.
  4. Identify a CPA experienced in real estate transactions and schedule a consultation before you sell — understanding your tax position in advance allows you to make informed decisions about timing and pricing.
  5. Retain all FSBO selling expense receipts (photography, flat-fee MLS, signage, legal fees, home staging) — these reduce your net sale proceeds and therefore reduce your taxable gain.

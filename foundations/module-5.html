Understanding Purchase Contracts

📚 Foundations Course⏱ 35 min read🏠 Module 5 of 6

The purchase contract is the legal document that governs your home sale from the moment an offer is accepted to the day of closing. It is typically 8–15 pages of dense legal language, written by attorneys for real estate boards, and designed to protect both parties from every scenario imaginable. Most FSBO sellers read it for the first time when an offer arrives — which is exactly the wrong time to encounter it. This module walks you through every key clause in plain language so you're never caught off guard by something you didn't understand.

What Is a Purchase Contract?

A residential purchase contract (also called a purchase and sale agreement, or PSA) is a legally binding contract between you (the seller) and the buyer that specifies the terms of the sale. Once both parties sign, you are under contract and the closing process begins. Breaking a signed purchase contract has legal and financial consequences for either party.

Most states have a standard form purchase contract developed by the state's real estate association or attorney general's office. Buyer's agents typically use the standard form for their state. As an FSBO seller, you should download your state's standard purchase contract form and read it in full before you receive your first offer. Familiarity with the document removes the stress of encountering it for the first time under negotiation pressure.

📋 Where to Get Your State's Contract

Search "[your state] residential purchase agreement PDF" or visit your state's real estate commission website. In many states, the form used by buyer's agents is published publicly. Alternatively, your title company or real estate attorney can provide the standard form used in your area.

Key Contract Elements Explained

Purchase Price and Earnest Money

The purchase price is the amount the buyer is offering for your home. This is the starting point for negotiation — it may not be your asking price, and you're not obligated to accept it as written.

Earnest money (also called a good faith deposit) is a sum the buyer deposits — typically within 1–3 business days of acceptance — to demonstrate their seriousness. Earnest money is typically 1–3% of the purchase price and is held in escrow by the title company or your state's approved escrow holder until closing, at which point it's applied to the buyer's down payment or closing costs.

What happens to earnest money if the deal falls apart depends on which party backs out and whether they had contractual grounds to do so. If the buyer backs out without a contractual contingency to protect them, you generally keep the earnest money. If they back out due to a valid contingency (inspection issues, financing failure, etc.), they typically get it back.

Closing Date

The closing date is when the transaction officially completes — when you transfer title and the buyer gets the keys. Most purchase contracts specify a closing date 30–45 days from the contract acceptance date, though cash sales can close in as few as 7–14 days and some financed transactions extend to 60 days.

The closing date is negotiable. If you need more time to find your next home, you can negotiate a later closing. If the buyer needs to close quickly, you can accommodate that in exchange for price or other concessions. Closing date flexibility is a negotiating chip on both sides.

Possession Date

Possession date is when the buyer takes physical possession of the property — when they get the keys. This is often (but not always) the same as the closing date. It is possible to negotiate a possession date that is days or even weeks after closing, allowing you more time to vacate. This arrangement is called a "post-closing occupancy agreement" or "rent-back" and is covered in more detail in Module 7.

The Three Major Contingencies

Contingencies are conditions that must be satisfied for the contract to proceed. If a contingency is not met, the buyer typically has the right to exit the contract and recover their earnest money. Understanding contingencies is essential — they are the primary source of deals falling apart.

1. Financing Contingency

The financing contingency (also called a mortgage contingency) protects the buyer if they cannot secure a loan to purchase your home. It gives the buyer a specified number of days (typically 21–30) to obtain a firm loan commitment from their lender. If the lender declines the loan for any reason, the buyer can exit the contract without penalty.

As a seller, you want this period to be as short as possible and to require the buyer to act diligently. Some buyers offer to waive the financing contingency (common in all-cash or very strong offers) — this is favorable to you but increases the buyer's risk.

2. Inspection Contingency

The inspection contingency gives the buyer the right to have the home professionally inspected within a specified timeframe (typically 7–14 days of acceptance). After reviewing the inspection report, the buyer can:

  • Accept the property as-is and proceed
  • Request that you make specific repairs before closing
  • Request a price reduction in lieu of repairs
  • Request a credit at closing to cover repair costs
  • Exit the contract entirely if they're unsatisfied with the inspection results

Inspection negotiations are where a disproportionate number of deals stall or fall apart. Module 10 (Complete tier) covers inspection negotiation strategy in depth.

3. Appraisal Contingency

If the buyer is using financing, the lender will require an independent appraisal of the property. The appraisal contingency protects the buyer if the home appraises for less than the purchase price. In this scenario, the lender will only finance up to the appraised value — the buyer must either make up the difference in cash, renegotiate the price with you, or exit the contract.

Appraisal contingencies can be waived by buyers willing to make up any gap between the purchase price and the appraised value. In hot markets with multiple offers, buyers sometimes waive the appraisal contingency to strengthen their offer. Understand what this means for you: if the home appraises short and the buyer has waived the appraisal contingency, they're committed to purchasing at the agreed price regardless.

Other Important Contract Clauses

Included and Excluded Items

The purchase contract specifies what's included in the sale (fixtures, appliances, window treatments) and what's explicitly excluded (the dining room chandelier you're taking to your new home, the garage refrigerator, the mounted TV in the bedroom). Be specific. What's attached to the home is generally considered a fixture and included in the sale by default — but you can exclude items by naming them. It's far better to address inclusions and exclusions explicitly upfront than to have a dispute on moving day.

Sale of Buyer's Current Home (Home Sale Contingency)

Some buyers condition their purchase on selling their own home first. This creates a chain of transactions — if their sale falls through, yours does too. Home sale contingencies are generally unfavorable to sellers in markets with multiple buyers. In slower markets, they're more common. If you accept an offer with a home sale contingency, consider including a "kick-out clause" that allows you to continue marketing the home and gives the buyer a window (typically 48–72 hours) to waive the contingency or lose the contract if you receive a better offer.

Seller Disclosures and Representations

The contract will typically reference the seller's disclosure form as a part of the agreement. Anything you know about the property's condition that could affect its value or the buyer's decision must be disclosed. Failure to disclose known defects can expose you to legal liability after closing. Module 9 (Complete tier) covers disclosure requirements in detail by state.

Default and Remedies

Every purchase contract includes provisions for what happens if either party defaults. If the buyer defaults without a valid contingency, you typically keep the earnest money as liquidated damages. If you default as the seller — by refusing to sell or accepting another offer after you're under contract — the buyer may be entitled to specific performance (a court forcing the sale) or damages. Don't enter into a contract you're not fully committed to completing.

Attorney Review Period

Some states (New Jersey, New York, Connecticut, Illinois, Massachusetts, and others) require or allow a period — typically 3–5 business days after contract signing — during which either party's attorney can review the contract and propose modifications or void the agreement. If you're in one of these states, be aware that a signed contract is not final until the attorney review period has expired.

Reviewing an Offer You've Received

When you receive an offer, don't react emotionally to the number. Evaluate the entire offer as a package. Consider:

  • Net to you after concessions: A $410,000 offer with $10,000 in closing cost assistance nets you $400,000. A $405,000 offer with no concessions nets more.
  • Financing vs. cash: Cash offers close faster, eliminate appraisal risk, and fall through less often. They're generally preferable even at a slightly lower price.
  • Contingencies: Fewer contingencies mean less risk that the deal collapses. Weigh contingency risk in your evaluation.
  • Closing date flexibility: Does the proposed closing date work for you? Does it give you enough time to find your next home?
  • Earnest money amount: A higher earnest money deposit signals buyer seriousness and increases the cost to them if they back out without cause.
💡 Use a Real Estate Attorney, Not Just the Contract

In most states, hiring a real estate attorney to review your contract is not required but is strongly recommended for FSBO sellers. Attorney fees for a transaction review typically run $500–$1,500 — a small cost relative to your transaction size. In states that use attorneys for closing rather than title companies, an attorney is already part of the standard process.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Read your state's standard purchase contract before you receive your first offer — familiarity removes negotiation pressure
  • Earnest money protects you if the buyer backs out without a valid contingency — 1–3% of purchase price is typical
  • The three major contingencies are financing, inspection, and appraisal — each gives the buyer an exit ramp under specific conditions
  • Evaluate offers as a complete package: net price after concessions, financing type, contingencies, and closing date flexibility all matter
  • Include and exclusion items must be specified explicitly — what's attached is generally included unless named otherwise
  • A real estate attorney review of your contract costs $500–$1,500 and is worth every dollar for FSBO sellers

✅ Action Steps

  1. Download your state's standard residential purchase agreement and read it in full before you list.
  2. Identify a real estate attorney in your area who handles residential closings — have their contact information ready before you receive an offer.
  3. Make a list of all items you plan to exclude from the sale (specific appliances, fixtures, or personal property) so you can address them clearly in any negotiation.
  4. Decide your position on contingencies in advance: what contingencies are you willing to accept, and what would cause you to push back?
  5. Complete Module 6 to understand what happens after you accept an offer — knowing the full closing process helps you negotiate closing dates and timelines confidently.

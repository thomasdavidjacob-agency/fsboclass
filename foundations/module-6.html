The Closing Process

📚 Foundations Course⏱ 30 min read🏠 Module 6 of 6

You've accepted an offer. You're under contract. Now what? The closing process is the 30–60 day period between contract acceptance and the day you hand over the keys and receive your proceeds. It involves multiple parties, dozens of tasks, and a significant amount of paperwork — and as an FSBO seller, you're coordinating much of it yourself. This module gives you a complete timeline, explains the role of every party involved, and prepares you for closing day so nothing surprises you.

The Closing Timeline: Day by Day

The closing process follows a relatively predictable sequence. Here's what typically happens in each phase of the 30–45 day window:

Days 1–3: Contract Executed and Earnest Money Deposited

Both parties sign the purchase contract. The buyer deposits earnest money — typically within 1–3 business days — into escrow with the title company. You should receive written confirmation that the deposit has been received. If the buyer misses the earnest money deadline without explanation, that's a red flag that warrants attention.

Days 1–7: Title Company/Attorney Opens Escrow

The title company (or real estate attorney in attorney-closing states) opens an escrow file. This is the neutral third party that holds funds and documents, coordinates the transaction, and issues title insurance. You'll receive a contact name and file number — keep this information handy for the duration of the transaction.

At this stage, the title company begins the title search — a review of public records to verify that you actually own the property, that there are no liens or encumbrances that would prevent you from selling it with clear title, and that no other parties have claims to the property.

Days 5–14: Home Inspection

The buyer schedules a professional home inspection. The inspector will spend 2–4 hours examining every accessible component of the home: foundation, roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, windows, insulation, and more. You don't need to be present, but you do need to provide access.

After the inspection, the buyer receives a report — typically 40–80 pages — and has a window (usually 7–14 days) to submit an inspection response. This response may request repairs, credits, or price reductions. Module 10 covers inspection negotiation in detail.

Days 14–21: Appraisal (Financed Purchases)

For financed purchases, the buyer's lender orders an appraisal. You don't select the appraiser — the lender assigns one from a rotating pool. The appraiser will schedule an appointment to visit the home, measure square footage, note condition, and compare it to recent sold properties to determine market value.

The appraisal result determines whether the lender will fund the loan at the agreed purchase price. If the appraiser values the home below the purchase price, you'll need to negotiate — either reduce the price, have the buyer make up the gap in cash, or a combination of both. If the appraisal comes in at or above the purchase price, you proceed.

Days 20–30: Loan Underwriting and Approval

The buyer's lender is simultaneously processing the loan application. Underwriting involves verifying the buyer's income, employment, assets, and debt — a process that involves considerable documentation and occasional unexpected requests. As the seller, your role here is minimal — but be responsive to any requests from the title company for additional documentation.

Common causes of underwriting delays: buyer changes jobs, makes a large purchase, or opens new credit during this period. All of these can jeopardize loan approval. Your transaction coordinator or title company will keep you informed of loan status milestones.

Days 25–30: Final Walkthrough

Buyers are typically entitled to a final walkthrough of the property within 24–48 hours of closing. The purpose is to verify that the home is in substantially the same condition as when they made the offer, that any agreed-upon repairs have been completed, and that all included items are still present.

This is not a second inspection — it's a condition check. Prepare for the final walkthrough by ensuring the home is clean, all personal property you're keeping has been removed, all agreed repairs are completed with receipts available, and all included appliances and fixtures are present and functional.

Days 28–45: Closing Day

Closing typically takes place at the title company's office (or real estate attorney's office in some states), though remote and mobile closings are increasingly available. The buyer and seller may sign simultaneously at the same table, or separately at different times during the day.

What You Sign at Closing

As the seller, you'll execute a smaller set of documents than the buyer (who signs the mortgage loan package). Here's what to expect:

  • Deed: The most important document. This transfers legal ownership of the property from you to the buyer. A closing officer or notary witnesses your signature. The deed is recorded with the county after closing.
  • Settlement Statement (ALTA/CD): A detailed accounting of all credits, debits, prorations, fees, and the final distribution of funds. Review this carefully — errors happen. You'll typically receive a preliminary version 24–48 hours before closing for review.
  • Seller's Affidavit: A sworn statement confirming that you're legally entitled to sell the property, that there are no undisclosed liens or judgments, and that you've occupied the home as represented.
  • Payoff Authorization: If you have a mortgage, this authorizes the title company to pay it off from your proceeds.
  • 1099-S Tax Form: The title company is required to report the sale to the IRS. You'll sign this at closing. Your tax advisor uses it when preparing your return — see Module 16 for tax implications.

Understanding Your Closing Costs as the Seller

As the FSBO seller, your closing costs are typically lower than a traditionally listed seller's because you're not paying a listing commission. Here's what you will still pay:

How Funds Are Transferred

On closing day, money moves in a very specific sequence:

  1. The buyer's lender wires the loan amount to the title company
  2. The buyer wires their down payment and closing costs to the title company
  3. The title company verifies that all funds are received
  4. Documents are signed and the deed is sent for recording
  5. Once recording is confirmed, the title company disburses funds: pays off your mortgage, pays transfer taxes, pays all vendors, and sends your net proceeds

Your net proceeds arrive either as a check handed to you at closing or, more commonly, wired to your bank account. If you wire funds preference, provide your banking information to the title company at least 48 hours before closing. Wire transfers typically arrive within a few hours of closing — same business day.

What to Bring to Closing

  • Government-issued photo ID (driver's license or passport)
  • Your social security number (for the 1099-S)
  • All keys, garage door openers, and access codes to the property
  • Security system codes and contact information
  • Any warranties, manuals, or receipts for appliances and systems you agreed to leave
  • Proof of any repairs completed (receipts and invoices)
  • Your bank account information for wire transfer of proceeds

After Closing: Your Responsibilities

Once the deed is recorded and proceeds are disbursed, the sale is final. A few things to address in the days and weeks after closing:

  • Cancel your homeowner's insurance — don't pay for coverage on a home you no longer own
  • Transfer utilities — notify all utility providers to close your accounts as of the closing date
  • Forward your mail — set up mail forwarding with USPS immediately
  • Keep your closing documents — store them permanently. You'll need the settlement statement for your tax return and potentially for future capital gains calculations.
  • Consult your tax advisor — closing day is a taxable event. See Module 16 for an overview of what to expect and what to bring to your CPA.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • The closing period runs 30–45 days and involves inspection, appraisal, underwriting, and final walkthrough — each with specific timelines
  • The title company is your neutral coordinator — they hold escrow funds, search title, prepare closing documents, and disburse proceeds
  • As the seller, your primary closing documents are the deed, settlement statement, seller's affidavit, and 1099-S
  • Your net proceeds are typically wired to your bank account on closing day — provide banking info 48 hours in advance
  • Seller closing costs as FSBO are typically 1–3% vs. 5–6% for traditional listings — the difference is your savings
  • After closing: cancel insurance, transfer utilities, forward mail, and keep all documents permanently for tax purposes

✅ Action Steps

  1. Identify the title company or real estate attorney you'll use for your closing — ask for a referral from neighbors who've sold recently, or search reviews in your area.
  2. Review a sample settlement statement (HUD-1 or ALTA form) online so you're familiar with the format before you see yours for real.
  3. Calculate your estimated net proceeds: sale price minus mortgage payoff, minus estimated closing costs, equals your check.
  4. Set up your banking information in advance — decide whether you want a wire transfer or check and notify the title company of your preference.
  5. Book a consultation with a CPA or tax advisor to understand the capital gains implications of your sale — ideally before you list, not after you close.

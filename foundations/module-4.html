Showing Your Home Safely

📚 Foundations Course⏱ 25 min read🏠 Module 4 of 6

Showing your home to strangers is a fundamentally different experience as an FSBO seller than it is as a seller represented by an agent. When you have an agent, they handle scheduling, qualify buyers, and are typically present during showings. As an FSBO seller, you're doing all of that yourself — and that requires a specific system for safety, efficiency, and turning showings into offers. This module gives you that system.

Qualifying Buyers Before You Show

Not every person who calls about your listing deserves a showing appointment. Many are curious neighbors, people at the very beginning of their home search with no financing in place, or even other agents trying to convert you into a client. Qualifying buyers before you let them in protects your time, your safety, and your home's security.

The Pre-Showing Qualification Questions

When a buyer or buyer's agent calls to schedule a showing, ask these questions before confirming an appointment:

  1. "Are you pre-approved for a mortgage?" Pre-approved — not just pre-qualified — means a lender has reviewed their income, assets, and credit and issued a conditional commitment. Pre-qualified means they answered a few questions online. These are not the same thing. Cash buyers should be prepared to provide proof of funds upon request.
  2. "What is your approximate price range?" If your home is listed at $450,000 and they're looking under $380,000, a showing wastes everyone's time.
  3. "What is your timeline?" A buyer who needs to move in six weeks is serious. A buyer who "is just starting to look" may not tour your home for another four months.
  4. "Are you currently working with a buyer's agent?" This matters for how you'll communicate and whether a buyer's agent commission is relevant to your situation.

You don't need to interrogate every caller. Keep it conversational. But these four questions separate motivated, qualified buyers from browsers — and your showing time is valuable.

⚠️ Fair Housing Reminder

Federal Fair Housing laws prohibit discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability. You may not decline showings or apply different standards based on any of these protected characteristics. Your qualification criteria must be uniformly applied to every inquiry. Stick to financial qualification and timeline — not personal characteristics of the buyer.

Safety Protocols for FSBO Showings

Home showing safety is a topic that real estate associations train agents on extensively — and FSBO sellers often overlook entirely. Here is a complete safety framework for showing your home as an owner-seller.

1. Never Show Alone If Possible

Whenever possible, have another adult present during showings. A spouse, family member, or trusted friend provides both a safety buffer and a practical benefit — you can split up so buyers can explore without feeling watched while someone remains visible.

If you must show alone, tell someone your schedule: who is coming, at what time, and when you expect them to leave. Share the buyer's name and phone number with a trusted contact before the showing begins.

2. Verify Identity Before Showing

Ask every buyer to confirm their first and last name when they arrive. If they're coming with a buyer's agent, verify the agent's license online (every state has a license lookup tool on the real estate regulatory website). For unrepresented buyers, it's entirely reasonable to request a copy of their pre-approval letter and confirm the name matches the person at your door before the showing.

3. Secure Valuables, Medications, and Documents

Before any showing, remove or lock away:

  • Prescription medications — pill theft is the most commonly reported theft during home showings
  • Jewelry, cash, and portable electronics
  • Financial documents, tax returns, mail with account numbers
  • Keys, including spare vehicle keys
  • Firearms and ammunition — store these locked and separately
  • Personal documents: passports, social security cards, checkbooks

This is not about distrusting every buyer. It's about eliminating risk entirely so you never have to wonder.

4. Control the Showing Flow

As you show the home, position yourself strategically. Never allow buyers to go to a part of the home where they're entirely out of your line of sight for extended periods. This is particularly relevant for basements, secondary floors, and detached structures like garages and workshops.

The standard showing flow is to start at the front door, move through the main living areas, then to bedrooms and bathrooms, then downstairs or to the garage. Maintain a natural conversational presence without hovering — buyers need space to envision the home as theirs.

5. Remove Pets During Showings

Regardless of how friendly your pets are, they create complications during showings. Some buyers are allergic, some are afraid of dogs, and all buyers are distracted by an animal competing for their attention. Remove pets — ideally by taking them to a neighbor's or boarding them for active showing days.

Scheduling Systems That Work

Managing showing requests manually — via text and phone — becomes chaotic quickly in an active market. Use a showing scheduling system from day one.

Recommended Tools

  • Calendly (free): Create a booking link with your available showing times. Buyers self-schedule without back-and-forth. Set 60–90 minute windows to allow each buyer adequate time and yourself buffer between appointments.
  • ShowingTime: The industry-standard showing scheduling tool used by most agents. Your flat-fee MLS service may give you access to ShowingTime, which automatically notifies you of requests and allows one-click confirmation.
  • Text-based scheduling: For lower-volume markets, managing by text is workable — but set a dedicated showing phone number (a Google Voice number is free) so your personal number isn't widely distributed.

Setting Showing Hours

Define your showing availability upfront and stick to it. A reasonable showing schedule for most markets:

  • Weekdays: 4:00 PM – 8:00 PM (after work hours)
  • Saturdays: 10:00 AM – 6:00 PM
  • Sundays: 12:00 PM – 5:00 PM

In hot markets, being more flexible increases your offer volume. In slower markets, your showing hours matter less — qualified buyers will accommodate you. Don't make yourself available 24/7. That's not sustainable and it signals desperation to agents who know better.

Open Houses vs. Private Showings

Open houses are a topic of significant debate among real estate professionals. Here's an honest assessment of their value for FSBO sellers:

The Case For Open Houses

Open houses create a sense of social proof and urgency. When buyers see other buyers touring a home simultaneously, their perception of desirability increases. An open house also allows you to show the home to multiple parties in a single day, reducing the coordination burden of individual appointments. For FSBO sellers who are still in the home, a Saturday open house can substitute for multiple private showings.

The Case Against (Or For Limited Use)

Research consistently shows that very few homes are actually sold through open houses. Most open house attendees are curious neighbors, early-stage browsers, and unqualified lookers. Serious, pre-approved buyers typically tour homes by private appointment — often with their buyer's agent. Open houses also create more pronounced security concerns because you're allowing multiple unverified strangers into your home simultaneously.

The Best Approach

Hold one open house in the first weekend of your listing — it generates initial momentum and exposure — then shift to private showings by appointment for qualified buyers. Do not hold weekly open houses indefinitely; it signals that you're having trouble selling.

Gathering and Using Feedback

After every showing, solicit feedback. This intelligence is valuable whether you're getting offers or not.

How to Ask for Feedback

Send a text or email within 24 hours of the showing: "Thank you for visiting [address]. We'd appreciate any feedback you're willing to share — what did you like most, and was there anything that gave you pause? Your thoughts help us a great deal."

For showings conducted through a buyer's agent, you can send the same message to the agent directly. Agents are more likely to give candid feedback than buyers, who may worry about being rude.

How to Interpret Feedback

Track your feedback in a simple log. After 3–5 showings, look for patterns:

  • Repeated price concerns: The market is telling you your price needs adjustment
  • Repeated condition concerns: Identify the specific issue and address it if possible
  • Repeated positive feedback but no offers: The home is well-received but buyers may be finding better value elsewhere — revisit your pricing
  • No feedback at all: The showing went poorly enough that buyers don't want to engage — consider what the experience felt like and whether anything can be improved
💡 The Pre-Showing Checklist

Run through this before every showing: fresh flowers or neutral scent, all lights on, temperature comfortable (68–72°F), all toilets flushed and lids closed, dishes out of the sink, beds made, personal photos away, pets removed, valuables secured, outdoor furniture arranged, driveway clear. This takes 20 minutes and meaningfully improves first impressions.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Qualify buyers with four key questions before confirming a showing: pre-approval, price range, timeline, and agent representation
  • Never show alone if possible — have a trusted adult present and always share showing details with someone in advance
  • Secure medications, valuables, firearms, and personal documents before every showing without exception
  • Use Calendly or ShowingTime to manage scheduling and avoid the back-and-forth chaos of manual coordination
  • Hold one open house in your first weekend to generate momentum, then shift to private showings by appointment
  • Track feedback from every showing — patterns in feedback reveal pricing or condition issues before they become deal-killers

✅ Action Steps

  1. Set up a Calendly account or Google Voice number for showing coordination before your listing goes live.
  2. Define your showing hours and include them in your listing description and any showing instructions.
  3. Create your pre-showing checklist and post it somewhere visible in your home so you can run through it quickly before each appointment.
  4. Secure a lockbox for the front door in case a buyer's agent needs to show while you're unavailable — this is optional but expands your showing flexibility.
  5. Create a simple feedback log (a notes app or spreadsheet works fine) to track themes from showing feedback.

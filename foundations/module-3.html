Writing Your Listing

📚 Foundations Course⏱ 28 min read🏠 Module 3 of 6

Your listing description is the single piece of written copy that does the heaviest lifting in your FSBO sale. It appears on the MLS, on Zillow, on Realtor.com, and on every platform that syndicates your listing. It is read by buyers at 11pm on their couch, scrolling through dozens of homes. In that context, your description needs to stop the scroll, create an emotional connection, and motivate a showing appointment — all in about 250 words. This module teaches you how to write one that does exactly that.

Why Most Listing Descriptions Fail

Read any 20 listing descriptions on Zillow in your area and you'll notice an alarming pattern. Most of them read like an inventory manifest: "3BR/2BA home with updated kitchen, hardwood floors, 2-car garage, large backyard, close to schools." That's a bullet list masquerading as a description. It tells buyers nothing they couldn't already see in the listing data fields. It creates no feeling, no aspiration, no urgency.

The homes that generate immediate showing requests are the ones whose descriptions make buyers feel something. They paint a picture of life in the home. They lead with the most compelling feature. They use sensory language. They don't bury the headline in generic adjectives.

Your goal is not to describe every room — buyers will see the photos. Your goal is to make them want to walk through the front door.

The Anatomy of a High-Performing Listing

Element 1: The Headline

Most listing platforms display a headline or property title at the top of your listing. Some platforms (like FSBO.com and Craigslist) give you full control over this. The MLS and Zillow sometimes auto-populate it from the property data, but where you have control, use it.

Your headline should answer the question: "Why is this home special?" Not "3BR/2BA in Maple Grove" — that's a description. A headline is a hook. Examples:

  • "Renovated Craftsman with Chef's Kitchen — Backs to Open Space"
  • "Corner Lot Colonial in Top-Rated School District — Move-In Ready"
  • "Rare Ranch Floor Plan with Finished Walkout Basement — No Stairs Living"

Notice that each headline identifies the home's key differentiator first. What makes your home special? Lead with that.

Element 2: The Opening Sentence

Your first sentence must hook the buyer. Don't open with the address. Don't open with "Welcome to this beautiful home." Open with the home's most compelling benefit — or better yet, a statement that makes the buyer envision themselves living there.

Instead of: "This beautiful 4-bedroom home features an updated kitchen."
Try: "Saturday morning breakfast tastes better in a kitchen like this — vaulted ceilings, quartz countertops, and natural light pouring through the windows overlooking a private backyard."

The second version is longer, but it creates a scene. Buyers buy feelings as much as features.

Element 3: The Feature Body

After your opening hook, you transition to the substance — the key features of your home. The rule here is simple: lead with benefits, not features. A feature is what something is. A benefit is what it means to the buyer.

  • Feature: "2-car garage." Benefit: "Two-car garage keeps both vehicles out of the elements year-round."
  • Feature: "Open floor plan." Benefit: "The open floor plan means the whole family stays connected — whether you're cooking, helping with homework, or watching the game."
  • Feature: "Walk-in closet." Benefit: "A true walk-in primary closet with built-ins — finally, enough space for both of you."

Write 3–5 benefit statements for your home's standout features. These form the body of your description.

Element 4: The Location Close

End your description with a location or lifestyle close — a sentence or two that connects your home to the community, schools, amenities, or lifestyle it enables. This is important because buyers aren't just buying a home; they're buying into a neighborhood and a way of life.

Examples: "Minutes from the Riverwalk, top-rated Lincoln Elementary, and easy highway access for commuters." Or: "Tucked at the end of a quiet cul-de-sac, yet five minutes from the town center."

Element 5: The Call to Action

Finish with a short, direct call to action: "Schedule your private showing today — this one won't last." Or: "Call or text to schedule. Flexible showing hours available." Include your contact phone number or email directly in the description wherever the platform allows it.

What Features to Highlight (By Priority)

Not all features are created equal. Buyers in most markets respond most strongly to the following, in rough order of impact:

  1. Kitchen: Updated kitchen is the single highest-impact feature in most markets. If yours is updated, lead with it.
  2. Primary bedroom/bathroom: The quality of the primary suite drives significant buying decisions, especially for move-up buyers.
  3. Outdoor space: Large lot, private backyard, deck, patio — anything that extends the living space outdoors.
  4. Natural light: Mention specific south- or east-facing orientation, skylights, or large windows if applicable.
  5. Storage: Walk-in closets, ample storage, basement, or attic space — buyers are chronically short on storage.
  6. Recent mechanical updates: New roof, HVAC, water heater, or windows reduce a buyer's anticipated capital expenditure. Mention specific year if recent.
  7. Garage and parking: In suburban and colder markets, garage is highly prioritized. State whether it's attached or detached, heated or not.
  8. Schools: If your home is in a high-rated district, say so explicitly. Many buyers filter by school district before anything else.

Keyword Strategy for the MLS

When your listing syndicates from the MLS to Zillow, Realtor.com, and other platforms, buyers search using keyword filters. Your written description contributes to search indexing. Include these specific terms naturally in your text:

  • Your neighborhood name (not just city)
  • School names ("Lincoln Elementary," "Westview High")
  • Nearby landmarks or attractions buyers care about
  • Specific material upgrades ("quartz," "hardwood," "stainless steel")
  • Structural features buyers search for ("single story," "no stairs," "finished basement," "main floor primary")

Do not keyword-stuff. Write naturally and include these terms where they make sense. Buyers read descriptions and a stuffed paragraph reads as amateurish and desperate.

What Photos to Lead With

Your listing photos appear in order. Most buyers see the first photo before they read a single word of your description. The order of your photos is a strategic decision, not a logistical one.

The Optimal Photo Order

  1. Photo 1: Exterior front — clean, well-lit, ideally blue-sky day. This is your hero image.
  2. Photos 2–3: Your most impressive interior space. Usually the kitchen or living room.
  3. Photos 4–5: Primary bedroom and bathroom.
  4. Photos 6–8: Remaining bedrooms, secondary bathrooms.
  5. Photos 9–10: Backyard, deck/patio, outdoor features.
  6. Photos 11+: Garage, laundry, basement, mechanical room, additional storage.
  7. Final photos: Neighborhood, street scene, proximity to parks or amenities.

Never lead with a bathroom, a bedroom closet, or a photo of the street. Lead with strength. Buyers form impressions in seconds, and the first three photos determine whether they keep scrolling or move on.

⚠️ The Photography Non-Negotiable

Smartphone photos are not acceptable for a home listing unless taken by someone who understands real estate photography angles, lighting, and staging. Dark, cluttered, or poorly composed photos will cost you more in lost buyer interest than a professional photographer charges. Budget $200–$400 for a professional shoot. It is the highest-ROI investment in your FSBO sale.

Staging Basics Before the Shoot

Your listing photos need to be taken in a home that is staged — not just clean, but intentionally arranged to photograph well and appeal to the broadest possible buyer pool. Here's the minimum staging checklist before your photo shoot:

  • Remove all personal photos, family memorabilia, and highly personal décor
  • Clear kitchen countertops of appliances, dishes, mail, and clutter — leave only 1–2 decorative items
  • Remove excessive furniture (most rooms have too much — less is more for photos)
  • Replace any burned-out light bulbs — bright rooms photograph larger
  • Make all beds with fresh, neutral linens
  • Remove items from shower floors, tubs, and toilet tops in bathrooms
  • Open all blinds and window treatments — maximize natural light
  • Remove pet items (bowls, beds, toys, litter boxes) from all visible areas
  • Clear the garage enough that at least one car can fit in the frame
  • Mow the lawn, trim hedges, remove garden hoses and tools from the yard

Common Listing Description Mistakes

Mistake 1: Writing in the Third Person About Your Own Home

"This charming home offers..." sounds like you're describing someone else's property. It's detached and impersonal. Either write in the second person ("Your new home features...") or use active, engaging language without a subject ("Vaulted ceilings and original hardwood throughout the main floor...").

Mistake 2: Overusing Meaningless Adjectives

"Gorgeous," "stunning," "beautiful," "amazing," "wonderful" — these words appear in virtually every listing and mean nothing. Replace them with specific, verifiable details. Don't say "gorgeous kitchen." Say "kitchen with custom cabinetry, quartzite countertops, and a 36-inch range."

Mistake 3: Listing Obvious Features

Don't describe things buyers can already see in the data fields. If your listing shows 4 bedrooms, don't write "four bedrooms." Use those words for something that adds value — how large the bedrooms are, whether any have walk-in closets, or how the layout positions them for privacy.

Mistake 4: Mentioning Problems

Never describe defects or issues in your listing description. You'll disclose them properly on your seller disclosure forms. Your listing description is marketing copy — it should present your home's genuine best features honestly. "Needs some TLC" or "sold as-is" in the listing description immediately signals problems and reduces your buyer pool.

Mistake 5: Failing to Mention FSBO Status Clearly

On platforms like Zillow FSBO, your status is already visible. But in the description itself, it helps to clarify contact instructions: "Owner-seller — call or text [number] for showings." This sets expectations for buyer's agents who may reach out regarding commission structure.

💡 Sample Listing Description Framework

Hook: "Weekend mornings start different when your kitchen looks like this."
Feature benefits: 3–4 sentences on standout features with benefit framing.
Supporting features: 2–3 bullet points for secondary features.
Location close: 1–2 sentences on neighborhood, schools, proximity.
CTA: "Call [number] to schedule your private tour today."
Total length: 200–300 words.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Your listing description's job is to motivate a showing — not to inventory every room
  • Lead with your home's single most compelling feature as the headline and opening hook
  • Write in benefits, not features: tell buyers what each feature means for their daily life
  • The first three photos determine whether buyers keep scrolling — lead with strength, always the exterior first
  • Professional photography is the highest-ROI investment in your FSBO — budget $200–$400 and don't skip it
  • Stage before you shoot: clear countertops, open blinds, remove personal items, maximize natural light

✅ Action Steps

  1. Write your listing headline — identify your home's single strongest differentiator and lead with it.
  2. Draft your listing description using the framework: hook → feature benefits → supporting details → location close → CTA.
  3. Complete your staging checklist before booking the photography shoot.
  4. Book a professional photographer — search "[your city] real estate photographer" and budget $200–$400.
  5. Review your final description for meaningless adjectives, obvious features, and anything that might signal a problem — cut or replace anything that doesn't add value.
